Load Balancing

One skill, learned today: place a load balancer in a design, pick its algorithm, and narrate what happens when a server behind it dies.

Why this matters

Lesson 0007 left a dangling question: requests "land on" A or B — who decides? The load balancer is the first box interviewers expect you to draw after the client, and "what does your LB do when a backend dies?" is a stock probe of whether you understand it or just draw it.

What it is

A load balancer (LB) is a server that sits in front of your fleet, presents one address to the world, and spreads incoming requests across the healthy backends. Because it stands between clients and servers relaying traffic, it's a kind of reverse proxy — nginx and HAProxy are the classic software LBs; every cloud sells one as a service [Cloudflare: load balancing].

Two operating layers, named after the network stack — a distinction interviewers like:

Health checks: how the LB knows

The LB's superpower is not sending traffic to corpses. It learns two ways: active checks — probe each backend every few seconds (GET /health, expect 200); passive checks — notice real requests failing or timing out. A failing backend is pulled from rotation, kept on probation, and re-added when checks pass again. Subtlety worth repeating in interviews: a health check answering 200 proves the process is up, not that it's correct — deeper checks (can it reach the DB?) catch more but can cause mass self-ejection when the DB blips. Doesn't the LB just move the problem?

Sharp question — the LB looks like a new single point of failure. Real deployments run at least two LBs with a shared virtual IP and failover, and DNS or anycast can spread across LB pairs. The full reliability story builds through phase 4; for now the interview-ready sentence is: "the LB is redundant itself — active-passive pair or DNS across multiple LBs."

AI-lab tie-in

Inference fleets lean on least connections-style balancing more than round robin: one request generating 4,000 tokens occupies a GPU replica for many seconds, while another finishes in one — exactly the "wildly varying request cost" case. Some LLM routers go further and balance on queue depth or KV-cache pressure per replica: same idea, richer signal.
