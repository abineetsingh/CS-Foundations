Queues & Deques

Today's skill: process things in arrival order at O(1) per operation — with the right container, because the obvious one is a trap.

Why this, now

Queues are the engine of breadth-first search — which powers a third of all tree and graph problems (Phases 2–3). Learn the container now so BFS lessons can focus on the algorithm.

FIFO, and the Python trap

A queue is First-In-First-Out: add at the back, remove from the front. You know from Lesson 03 why a plain list fails here: pop(0) shifts everything — O(n) per dequeue, O(n²) to drain a queue. The right tool:

from collections import deque

q = deque()
q.append(x)         # enqueue at back  — O(1)
x = q.popleft()     # dequeue at front — O(1)
q.appendleft(x)     # and the front works too — O(1)
x = q.pop()         # both ends, both O(1): a Double-Ended QUEue

deque is a doubly-linked structure of blocks — no shifting, ever (Python Wiki). The catch: q[i] random access is O(n), the exact opposite of a list. Choose by which operations dominate.

The BFS preview

Here's the skeleton you'll use dozens of times from Lesson 20 on — a queue guarantees you explore things in the order you discovered them, level by level:

q = deque([start])
while q:
    current = q.popleft()      # oldest discovery first
    for neighbor in expand(current):
        q.append(neighbor)     # discoveries wait their turn

Swap the deque for a stack and the same skeleton becomes depth-first search. The container choice is the algorithm — a beautiful fact you'll prove to yourself in Phase 3.

Level 2: the monotonic deque (recognition only)

Lesson 07 left a loose end: the maximum of a sliding window can't be updated naively when the max leaves. The fix is a deque holding candidate maxima in decreasing order — new elements evict smaller ones from the back (they can never be max again), stale ones fall off the front. O(n) total. You don't need to reproduce it yet; you need to recognize "sliding window maximum" as a monotonic-deque problem and know why a plain summary fails.

Mission link · ML/AI

Inference servers batch incoming generation requests with queues — continuous batching (vLLM) is queue management under a latency SLO. Data-loading pipelines are producer/consumer queues feeding the GPU. When a paper says "requests are scheduled FIFO," you now know both the policy and the container.
