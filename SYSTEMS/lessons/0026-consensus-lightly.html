Consensus, Lightly

One skill, learned today: explain why machines need to vote, what quorums are, and what Raft/ZooKeeper/etcd are for — no protocol proofs required.

Why this matters

Since lesson 0015 you've said "promote a follower" as if it were easy. It isn't — deciding which follower, when half the machines can't see the other half, is one of computing's genuinely hard problems. Interviews don't ask you to derive Raft; they ask what breaks without it ("split brain?") and what tools embody it. That's exactly today's scope.

The nightmare: split brain

Failover (0015) hides a trap. Followers detect the leader is "dead" — but per lesson 0022, silence is ambiguous: dead, or just partitioned away? Guess wrong and you get split brain: two leaders, each accepting writes, diverging with every second. Reconciling two histories of a bank ledger isn't an engineering task; it's an apology tour. Quorums: majority rules, arithmetic edition

The fix is voting with a quorum — a majority: ⌊N/2⌋ + 1 of N nodes (3 of 5, 2 of 3). Decisions (like "who is leader") require quorum agreement. The magic is pure arithmetic: two disjoint majorities of the same set cannot exist — any two quorums share at least one member, so two leaders can't both win an election, no matter how the network splits. Consequences worth reciting:

  • Odd cluster sizes (3, 5, 7): 4 nodes tolerate 1 failure (quorum 3) — same as 3 nodes. The 4th buys nothing but cost.
  • Minority side goes read-only/down — that's CAP's CP choice (0018) made mechanical: better a stalled minority than a second leader.
  • Quorum writes/reads (Dynamo-style, W + R > N) apply the same overlap trick to data: any read set intersects any write set, so a read always meets the newest value.

What Raft is, and the tools that bottle it

Raft (and Paxos before it) is a consensus protocol: the full machinery for a cluster to elect leaders by quorum, replicate a log of decisions, and survive any minority of failures — provably, including the fiendish edge cases (simultaneous candidates, dueling timeouts, log conflicts). The practical career fact: you will almost never implement consensus; you will use it bottled:

ToolWhat it isYou use it for
etcd / ZooKeepera small, Raft/ZAB-replicated key-value store — a consensus serviceleader election, distributed locks, service discovery, cluster config. Kubernetes stores its entire state in etcd (see 0033)
Managed databases' failoverconsensus embedded in the productPostgres HA setups, MongoDB replica sets, Kafka controllers (KRaft) — your 0015 "promote a follower" is one of these voting internally

Design instinct to carry: consensus is expensive (every decision = round trips to a quorum — your 0005 latency math). So architectures put tiny, critical facts behind consensus (who leads; where shards live) and keep bulk data on cheaper machinery (async replication, caches). Small strong core, big eventual body.

AI-lab tie-in

A distributed training run has exactly one precious fact: which checkpoint/step is authoritative when nodes die mid-run. Training orchestrators keep job state and worker membership in etcd-like stores — consensus guarding the tiny truth, while the petabytes of gradients fly over cheap, unguarded channels. Same small-core/big-body split.
