Back-of-Envelope Capacity Estimation

One skill, learned today: turn "10 million users" into QPS, storage, and bandwidth numbers — in about ninety seconds, out loud.

Why this matters

Minutes 5–10 of a system design interview are usually estimation. Its real purpose isn't the numbers — it's that the numbers drive the design: 500 QPS needs one good database; 500,000 QPS needs sharding and caches. Interviewers watch whether your arithmetic changes your architecture [System Design Primer].

The four formulas

Everything reduces to these, plus the courage to round aggressively (this is estimation — 86,400 is 10⁵, π is 3):

QuantityFormulaNotes
QPS (avg)daily actions ÷ 10⁵ sseconds per day ≈ 86,400 ≈ 10⁵
QPS (peak)avg × 2–10traffic is bursty; state your multiplier
Storageitems/day × size × days kept × replicasreplicas ×3 is the usual default (lesson 0015)
BandwidthQPS × payload sizeseparate read vs write paths

Two more habits: establish the read:write ratio first (most systems are 10:1 to 100:1 read-heavy — it decides where the pressure is), and keep units honest: KB→MB→GB→TB is ×1000 each step; a character is a byte; an id is 8 bytes; a timestamp is 8 bytes.

Worked example: Twitter-lite

Given: 10 M DAU. Each user reads 100 tweets/day, posts 1/day. Tweet ≈ 280 chars + metadata ≈ 0.5 KB. Keep 5 years.

Writes:  10M × 1 post   = 10⁷/day  ÷ 10⁵s ≈ 100 QPS avg   → ~500 peak    (easy)
Reads:   10M × 100 reads = 10⁹/day ÷ 10⁵s ≈ 10,000 QPS avg → ~50,000 peak (the hard part)
         read:write = 100:1 → design for reads: caches, replicas

Storage: 10⁷ tweets/day × 0.5 KB = 5 GB/day
         × 365 × 5 yrs ≈ 9 TB × 3 replicas ≈ ~27 TB       (modest! text is small)

Read bandwidth: 10⁴ QPS × 0.5 KB ≈ 5 MB/s avg              (trivial — unless images enter)

Notice the conclusions falling out: writes are easy, reads need help (caching, lesson 0009; replicas, 0015), storage is fine until images arrive (object storage, 0019). That's estimation doing its job.
