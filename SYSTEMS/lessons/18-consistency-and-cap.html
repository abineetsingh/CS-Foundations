Consistency Models & CAP

One skill, learned today: state precisely what guarantee your reads have — and handle the CAP theorem without reciting the caricature.

Why this matters

You've been paying an IOU since lesson 09: "stale," "eventually consistent," "read-your-writes" — used, not defined. Interviews cash this check directly ("is your design strongly consistent? where does it need to be?"), and CAP is the most name-dropped — and most misquoted — theorem in the field. You'll be the one quoting it right.

The spectrum of promises

A consistency model is a contract about what reads may return in a replicated system. Ordered strongest to weakest [Jepsen: consistency models]:

ModelThe promiseFeels like
Linearizable ("strong")every read sees the latest completed write, system-wideone single database, no copies — the illusion is total
Read-your-writesyou always see your own writes; others may laglesson 15's fix, now with its formal name
Monotonic readsyou never see time go backwards (data appearing then vanishing)refresh never "un-shows" a comment
Eventualstop writing, and replicas converge… eventuallythe weakest useful promise — and often plenty

Stronger = simpler to program against, slower and less available. Weaker = fast and resilient, but every consumer must tolerate anomalies. The design skill is the one you've been rehearsing since caching: classify each data path by the weakest model it can tolerate. Balances: linearizable. Timelines: eventual. Profile edits: read-your-writes.

CAP, stated correctly

The theorem [IBM: CAP theorem]: when a network partition cuts your replicas off from each other — and given enough time it will — each side must choose: refuse requests (stay Consistent, lose Availability) or keep answering (stay Available, risk divergent/stale data). That's it. Not "pick 2 of 3 at the store" — P isn't optional, and the choice only bites during partitions:

Two upgrades that mark a strong candidate: (1) CP/AP is per-operation, not per-system — your checkout can refuse while your browse stays up. (2) PACELC: even with no partition (Else), you still trade Latency vs Consistency — waiting for replicas to confirm every write is the price of strong reads on them. Partitions are rare; the E-L-C trade runs 24/7.

AI-lab tie-in

Rate-limit counters for an LLM API are a textbook consistency choice: count linearizably (every token check hits one consistent store — accurate, slow, and it melts) or eventually (each region counts locally, syncs async — fast, and a user can briefly exceed their quota during a partition). Every provider picks eventual and eats the overshoot. Now you know the vocabulary they decided with.
