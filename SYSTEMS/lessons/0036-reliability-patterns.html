Reliability Patterns

One skill, learned today: read the anatomy of a cascading failure — and place the three defenses that stop it: circuit breakers, bulkheads, graceful degradation.

Why this matters

Big outages are rarely one thing breaking — they're one small thing breaking and the failure propagating. "How does your design fail?" is the deepest interview question there is, and these three patterns are its expected vocabulary. They're also the SRE-book material (0035's SLOs told you when to care; today is how to survive).

Anatomy of a cascade

You've met the ingredients separately; now watch them conspire. A minor dependency slows down → callers' threads pile up waiting (no timeout, or too-generous ones — 0022) → the callers exhaust their pools and slow down too → their callers pile up → retries multiply the load (0028's storm) → healthy services drown in traffic meant for the sick one. One slow non-critical feature takes down the whole product [Google SRE: cascading failures]:

The three defenses

PatternMechanismProtects against
Circuit breakerwrap calls to a dependency; count failures. Too many → the breaker opens: calls fail instantly for a cooldown, no network attempt. After the cooldown, half-open: let a few probes through; success closes it, failure re-openswasting threads on a dead dependency; hammering it while it recovers (the recovery-prevention loop)
Bulkheadpartition resources per dependency: separate connection pools / thread pools / pod fleets (ship-compartment metaphor)one dependency's stall consuming ALL your threads — recommendation-service slowness can only sink the recommendation compartment
Graceful degradationa designed fallback per feature: cached/stale data (0009), a default, or hiding the feature — decided at design time, not improvised at 3ambinary all-or-nothing failure; converts "site down" into "recommendations missing"

They compose into one sentence you should be able to say fluently: "Calls to each dependency get timeouts and a circuit breaker, run from a bulkheaded pool, and every non-critical dependency has a designed fallback — so a failing dependency costs us its feature, never the product." The mindset shift interviewers listen for: stop asking if dependencies fail; budget for when.

One more habit worth naming: test the armor. Chaos engineering — deliberately killing pods and injecting latency in production-like conditions (Netflix's Chaos Monkey made it famous) — exists because untested fallbacks rot: the cache path nobody exercised for a year is itself broken when the 3am cascade finally calls on it [Principles of Chaos].

AI-lab tie-in

LLM products degrade gracefully along a quality axis most software lacks: primary model overloaded → fall back to a smaller/faster model, shrink context, or queue the request — worse answers beating no answers. Provider APIs are bulkheaded per-tenant (one customer's burst can't starve others' capacity), and every serious agent framework wraps tool calls in circuit breakers — a flaky tool must not wedge the whole agent loop.
