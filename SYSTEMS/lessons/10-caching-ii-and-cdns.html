Caching II: Invalidation, Stampedes & CDNs

One skill, learned today: name the two ways caches betray you — staleness and stampedes — and the standard defenses for each. Plus: the cache that wraps the planet.

Why this matters

"There are only two hard things in computer science: cache invalidation and naming things" — the joke survives because it's true. Interviewers who see a cache on your whiteboard will ask "what happens when the data changes?" and "what happens when a hot key expires?" Today you get real answers.

Problem 1: staleness — and invalidation strategies

The note gets edited; the cache still holds the old copy. Your options, in the order to reach for them:

StrategyHowCost
TTL onlylet entries expire; tolerate bounded stalenesssimplest; staleness up to TTL — fine for feeds, fatal for balances
Invalidate on writeon update: write DB, then DELETE note:17 from cachenext reader misses and refills fresh; small race windows remain under concurrency
Write-throughon update: write DB and overwrite the cache entrycache always warm; you now maintain two writes and their ordering
Event-driven purgedata changes emit events; consumers purge keysthe scalable version — needs queues (lesson 23)

The interview-ready default: cache-aside + TTL + delete-on-write — and note the golden detail that you delete rather than update the cache on writes, letting the next read repopulate; updating in place invites subtle write-ordering races [AWS Builders' Library].

Problem 2: the stampede

A hot key's TTL expires. In the next 100 ms, 5,000 readers all miss simultaneously — and all 5,000 charge into the database with the same query. The DB, sized for 1% miss traffic (lesson 09), tips over; its slowness causes more timeouts and retries; the failure spreads. Also called thundering herd or dog-piling. Watch it, then the fix:

The defenses, usually combined:

  • Request coalescing / locking: only one miss per key is allowed through to the DB; the other 4,999 wait ~10 ms for it to fill the cache.
  • TTL jitter: expire at 300s ± random(30s), so many keys loaded together don't all expire together.
  • Stale-while-revalidate: keep serving the expired copy briefly while one background fetch refreshes it — users get speed, DB gets one query [web.dev].

The CDN: caching, weaponized geographically

A CDN (content delivery network — Cloudflare, CloudFront, Akamai) is a fleet of cache servers in hundreds of cities. Users hit the nearest edge location (~10 ms away) instead of your origin (~100+ ms away); the edge serves from cache or fetches from origin once and reuses it for every nearby user [Cloudflare: what is a CDN]. Two facts to carry:

  • It's the same machinery you just learned — hit ratios, TTLs, purges — just placed at planetary scale. You control it with the Cache-Control response header (max-age, no-store…) [MDN: HTTP caching].
  • Static assets (images, JS, video) are the classic cargo; modern CDNs also cache whole API responses and HTML where staleness allows. In interviews: any "serve images/video globally" requirement should trigger the word CDN within seconds.
AI-lab tie-in

Model-serving fleets fight stampedes too: a cold model or an evicted KV-cache prefix plays the role of the expired hot key, and a burst of identical prompts plays the herd. The fixes rhyme: coalesce identical in-flight work, keep popular prefixes warm, spread expiries. Cache thinking transfers whole.
